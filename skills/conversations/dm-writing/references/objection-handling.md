# Objection Handling Library

Complete reference for handling every common objection in LinkedIn DMs. The instinct when someone objects is to push harder or over-explain. That is almost always wrong. Objections are signals that something feels unsafe. Your job: find what's behind it and make them feel more safe.

## The Pattern (Every Objection)

1. **Acknowledge** — validate their concern, don't dismiss it
2. **Ask what's behind it** — the stated objection is rarely the real one
3. **Address the real concern** — with specifics, not generalities
4. **Give an easy out** — they should feel MORE safe after your reply

## Objection: "I'll Think About It"

**What's behind it:** Risk outweighs perceived value. They don't feel safe enough yet.

**Do:** Reduce risk. Offer a smaller next step, share a relevant result, or ask what specifically they'd want to think about.

**Don't:** Push for urgency. Create false scarcity. Ask "what's holding you back?"

### Good Example

> **Them:** "Sounds interesting. Let me think about it."
>
> **You:** Totally fair -- no rush at all. If it helps, the thing most people in your position want to know is whether this actually works for [their specific situation]. I can send you a quick breakdown of what we did for [similar company] -- takes 2 minutes to read. Would that help while you're thinking it over?

**Why it works:** Acknowledges their need for time. Doesn't push. Offers a low-effort next step addressing the likely concern (will it work for me?). Easy out.

### Bad Example

> **Them:** "Let me think about it."
>
> **You:** Sure! But honestly the spots are filling up fast and I'd hate for you to miss out. Can we just book a quick call to discuss?

**Why it fails:** Manufactured urgency. Ignores stated need. Pushes bigger commitment when they asked for space.

### Draft Notes

```
ai_notes: "Objection: 'I'll think about it.' Likely concern: not sure it works for their situation. Sent [relevant proof/example]. Low-pressure next step offered."
```

## Objection: "Too Expensive" / "What Does It Cost?"

**What's behind it:** Value isn't clear yet. They need to see the outcome before the price makes sense.

**Do:** Reframe around the cost of the problem, not the cost of the solution. Show the gap between what they're losing now and what they'd gain.

**Don't:** Lead with the number. Offer discounts immediately. Get defensive about pricing.

### Good Example

> **Them:** "That seems expensive."
>
> **You:** I get it -- it's an investment. Just curious though, you mentioned you're losing 30% of new hires in the first 3 months. At your scale, what does that cost in recruiting and training fees alone? Most teams we work with find the numbers are pretty eye-opening when they add it up.

**Why it works:** Validates the concern. Reframes to cost of inaction. Uses their own data. Lets them do the math themselves.

### Bad Example

> **Them:** "That seems expensive."
>
> **You:** Actually, we're one of the most affordable options in the market! I can offer you a 15% discount if you sign up this week.

**Why it fails:** Devalues the offer. Discounting before building value. Creates pressure.

## Objection: "Bad Timing" / "I'm Busy"

**What's behind it:** They don't feel the cost of waiting. Inaction feels safer than action.

**Do:** Make the cost of doing nothing tangible. Don't pressure on timing -- help them see what stays the same if they wait.

**Don't:** Say "when would be a better time?" (vague). Push for urgency. Minimize their schedule.

### Good Example

> **Them:** "Not a great time right now."
>
> **You:** Totally understand. Just curious -- you mentioned the ramp-time issue was costing you about 6 figures a year. If nothing changes in the next 6 months, what does that look like for your Q3 hiring plan? No pressure either way -- just want to make sure the timing decision is an informed one.

**Why it works:** Acknowledges their reality. Makes the cost of waiting concrete using their own numbers. No pressure. Easy out.

### Bad Example

> **Them:** "Not a great time."
>
> **You:** No worries! When would be better? I can follow up next month. Or maybe we could just do a quick 10-minute call to keep the ball rolling?

**Why it fails:** Vague timing. Still pushing for a call. Doesn't address the real issue.

## Objection: "I've Tried This Before"

**What's behind it:** They got burned. Trust is low because of a past experience. This is the hardest objection because it's based on evidence.

**Do:** Validate their experience fully. Don't dismiss it. Then differentiate with specifics about how your approach differs on the exact dimension where they got burned.

**Don't:** Say "we're different." Trash the competitor. Minimize their past experience.

### Good Example

> **Them:** "We tried something similar last year and it didn't work."
>
> **You:** That's frustrating, and honestly pretty common. Do you mind sharing what specifically didn't work? I ask because there's a specific failure mode I see a lot in this space, and knowing where it broke down would help me tell you honestly whether what we do would be any different.

**Why it works:** Validates their experience. Shows curiosity, not defensiveness. Promises honesty, not a sales pitch. Creates space for them to share the real issue.

### Bad Example

> **Them:** "Tried something similar. Didn't work."
>
> **You:** Oh yeah, those other solutions are nothing like what we do. Our approach is completely different and way more effective!

**Why it fails:** Dismissive. No curiosity. Unsubstantiated claim. They'll assume you're just another version of what burned them.

## Objection: "Not Sure It's For Me"

**What's behind it:** They can't see themselves in what you're describing. The identity match is weak.

**Do:** Get more specific about who you help. Share an example of someone in their exact situation -- same role, same company size, same industry.

**Don't:** Make it more generic. List features. Say "it works for everyone."

### Good Example

> **Them:** "Not sure this is really for someone in my position."
>
> **You:** That's fair. Just for context -- the person I worked with most recently who was in almost your exact spot was a [their role] at a [their company type], about [their size]. Their main issue was [their problem]. Does that sound at all familiar, or is your situation different?

**Why it works:** Extremely specific match. Lets them self-identify. Asks if it resonates rather than insisting it applies.

## Objection: "Send Me More Info"

**What's behind it:** Interested but not ready to commit to a conversation. Needs one more reason to engage.

**Do:** Send ONE specific, relevant thing -- not a brochure, not a full website, not "everything we do." Then follow up with a question about that specific thing.

**Don't:** Send a PDF deck. Send three links. Send your full website. Treat it as a sale.

### Good Example

> **Them:** "Can you send me more info?"
>
> **You:** Sure -- rather than sending the whole overview, here's the one piece that's most relevant to what you mentioned about [their specific situation]: [specific link or 2-sentence summary]. Curious if that resonates with what you're seeing on your end.

**Why it works:** Curated, not generic. Tied to their situation. Ends with a question that keeps the conversation going.

### Bad Example

> **Them:** "Send me some info."
>
> **You:** Absolutely! Here's our website: [link]. And here's our case study deck: [link]. And our pricing page: [link]. Let me know what you think!

**Why it fails:** Overwhelming. No curation. No question. All take, no give.

## Quick Reference Table

| Objection | Real Concern | Response Strategy | Follow-Up Reminder |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| "I'll think about it" | Risk > value | Smaller next step + relevant proof | 3-5 days |
| "Too expensive" | Value unclear | Reframe: cost of problem vs solution | 5-7 days |
| "Bad timing" | Cost of waiting unclear | Make inaction tangible | 7-14 days |
| "Tried before" | Past burn, low trust | Validate, ask what broke, differentiate | 5-7 days |
| "Not for me" | Identity mismatch | Specific example of someone like them | 3-5 days |
| "Send info" | Needs one more reason | ONE specific thing + follow-up question | 3 days |
