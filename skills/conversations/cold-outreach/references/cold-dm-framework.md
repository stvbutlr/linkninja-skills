# Cold DM Framework

Deep reference for crafting cold DMs. The specificity-personalization grid, language mirroring rules, and permission close structure.

## The Specificity-Personalization Grid

Every cold DM sits on two axes. Your goal is always the top-right quadrant:

| | Low Personalization | High Personalization |
|---|---|---|
| **High Specificity** | Useful but impersonal. Shows expertise but feels like a template. Moderate replies. | **The sweet spot.** Specific to their situation AND personalized to them. Highest reply rate. |
| **Low Specificity** | Generic template spam. "I help companies grow." Lowest reply rate. | Friendly but vague. "Hey! Love your profile!" No substance. Low reply rate. |

### Specificity Levels

| Level | Example | Approximate Reply Rate |
|-------|---------|----------------------|
| Zero | "I help companies with sales" | ~1% |
| Low | "I help SaaS companies with sales" | ~3% |
| Medium | "I help B2B SaaS teams post-Series B" | ~8% |
| High | "I help B2B SaaS teams scaling from 50 to 200 headcount post-Series B with onboarding" | 15%+ |

The test: could this message work for 100 people, or only this one person?

### Personalization Sources

| Signal | Where to Find It | How to Use It |
|--------|------------------|---------------|
| Their headline | Profile | Mirror their exact words |
| Recent post | Activity feed | Reference the specific topic |
| Company news | LinkedIn or news | "Congrats on the Series B" |
| Mutual connection | Network | Social proof: "[Name] and I connected over..." |
| Shared background | Profile | Authentic connection point |

## Language Mirroring Rules

People trust who sounds like them. Language mirroring is the fastest trust signal in a cold DM.

### The 5 Language Layers

When reading a prospect's profile and posts, extract language from these layers:

| Layer | What It Is | Example | Use In DM |
|-------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Functional** | Industry terms for what they do daily | "pipeline velocity," "sprint planning," "deal desk" | Use their exact terms, not synonyms |
| **Technical** | Tools and metrics they reference | "HubSpot sequences," "ARR," "MRR churn" | Show you know their stack |
| **Cultural** | How their industry talks informally | "eating our own dog food," "drinking from the firehose" | Signals insider status |
| **Psychic** | Emotional language about their challenges | "drowning in," "keeping the lights on," "herding cats" | Mirror frustration or aspiration |
| **Narrative** | The story they tell about their role | "building from scratch," "turning things around" | Reference their journey |

### Stacking Rule

Use 2-3 layers per message. More than 3 feels forced. Zero feels generic.

**Good stacking (Functional + Psychic):**
> "Most teams scaling past 50 SDRs say ramp time is the thing keeping them up at night."

**Over-stacking (all 5):**
> "As someone building demand gen from scratch at a Series B using HubSpot while drinking from the firehose on this scaling journey..." — too much.

### Anti-Patterns

| Don't | Why |
|-------|-----|
| Use marketing vocabulary ("synergy," "leverage," "solutions") | Signals outsider — you sound like a brochure |
| Correct their terminology | Condescending — use their words even if technically imprecise |
| Stack all 5 layers | Feels try-hard and performative |
| Use jargon from the wrong subculture | Worse than generic — signals you're faking familiarity |

## Permission Close Structure

Every cold DM ends with permission, not pressure. The person who needs the outcome less wins.

### The Permission Spectrum

| Pressure Level | Example | Effect |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| High pressure | "Let's book a call this week" | Trust withdrawal. They retreat. |
| Medium pressure | "Can I send you more info?" | Neutral — still assumes interest |
| Low pressure (target) | "Would you be open to me sharing that?" | Safe. Easy to say yes or no. |
| Zero pressure | "Just thought you'd find that interesting" | No friction, but no next step |

Target low pressure. It creates the most replies because saying "yes" feels safe and saying "no" is easy.

### Good Permission Closes

- "Would that be useful?"
- "Happy to share if relevant — no pressure either way."
- "Let me know if that'd help."
- "Mind if I share a quick framework?"

### Bad Closes

- "Let's hop on a call" — assumes interest
- "When's a good time?" — assumes agreement
- "I'd love to tell you more" — self-focused
- "Are you the right person?" — puts them on the spot

## The 3 Risks to Reduce

Every cold DM creates 3 types of risk for the recipient. Reduce all three:

| Risk | What They Feel | How to Reduce |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| **Time risk** | "Will this waste my time?" | Keep it short. Specific ask. "Takes 2 minutes to read." |
| **Ego risk** | "Will I look stupid if I engage?" | No obligation. Easy out. Frame as curiosity, not need. |
| **Social risk** | "Will others judge me?" | Professional tone. Credible signal. Nothing salesy. |

Short ask + clear context + permission-based language + no obligation tone = all three risks minimized.
